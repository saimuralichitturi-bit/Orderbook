# Order Book Session Memory
> Complete financial framework to determine pure value from an order book using Revenue and Market Cap

---

## The Core Question
```
A company announces a new order of ₹X Crore
→ Is it BIG or SMALL for that company?
→ Will it move the stock?
→ Does it create real value?
```
The answer is never absolute — it is always RELATIVE to two anchors:
1. Annual Revenue (how much work they can do)
2. Market Cap (what the market already expects)

---

## The Two Anchor Formulas

### Formula 1 — Order Book vs Revenue
```
Coverage Ratio = Order Book Value / Annual Revenue (TTM)
```

| Ratio | Signal | Meaning |
|-------|--------|---------|
| < 1x | WEAK ❌ | Less than 1 year of revenue lined up |
| 1x – 2x | STABLE ⚠️ | Adequate but not exciting |
| 2x – 4x | GOOD ✅ | Healthy pipeline, 2-4 years visibility |
| 4x – 6x | STRONG ✅ | Excellent — stock re-rating likely |
| > 6x | INVESTIGATE ⚠️ | Too high — check execution capacity |

### Formula 2 — Order Book vs Market Cap
```
Market Cap Coverage = Order Book Value / Market Cap × 100
```

| Coverage % | Signal | Meaning |
|------------|--------|---------|
| > 100% | DEEP VALUE ✅ | Order book > market cap — market hasn't priced the pipeline |
| 30% – 100% | SIGNIFICANT ✅ | Strong re-rating candidate |
| 10% – 30% | MODERATE ⚠️ | Meaningful but not transformative |
| < 10% | NOISE ❌ | Market won't react |

**Rule: Always check BOTH. One alone will mislead you.**

## The 6 Pure Financial Test Cases

---

### Test Case 1 — Small Company, Giant Order (Re-rating Event)
```
Company   : MTAR Tech
Revenue   : ₹100 Cr
Market Cap: ₹1,000 Cr
Order     : ₹400 Cr

Coverage Ratio : 400 / 100 = 4x        → STRONG ✅
Market Cap %   : 400 / 1000 × 100 = 40% → SIGNIFICANT ✅

Verdict: STOCK RE-RATING EVENT 🟢
Both checks confirm — this order is transformative for the company.
Market will re-price the stock upward as future revenue is now visible.
```

---

### Test Case 2 — Same Order, Big Company (Pure Noise)
```
Company   : L&T
Revenue   : ₹2,00,000 Cr
Market Cap: ₹4,00,000 Cr
Order     : ₹400 Cr

Coverage Ratio : 400 / 200000 = 0.002x  → NOISE ❌
Market Cap %   : 400 / 400000 × 100 = 0.1% → NOISE ❌

Verdict: IGNORE 🔴
Same ₹400 Cr order that moved MTAR 15%
will not move L&T even 0.1%.
Scale is everything.
```

---

### Test Case 3 — Order Book Greater Than Market Cap (Deep Value)
```
Company   : J. Kumar Infraprojects
Revenue   : ₹4,500 Cr
Market Cap: ₹6,780 Cr
Order Book: ₹21,011 Cr

Coverage Ratio : 21011 / 4500 = 4.7x     → STRONG ✅
Market Cap %   : 21011 / 6780 × 100 = 310% → DEEP VALUE ✅

Verdict: DEEP UNDERVALUATION 🟢
Order book is 3.1x the market cap.
Market is pricing the company at far less than its committed future revenue.
Classic re-rating opportunity in infrastructure sector.
```
*Source: Trade Brains research on Indian infra stocks*

---

### Test Case 4 — High Ratio But Execution Impossible (Trap)
```
Company   : Small EPC Startup
Revenue   : ₹20 Cr
Capacity  : ₹30 Cr/year (max)
Market Cap: ₹200 Cr
Order     : ₹300 Cr

Coverage Ratio : 300 / 20 = 15x        → Looks incredible ✅
Market Cap %   : 300 / 200 × 100 = 150% → Looks incredible ✅

BUT:
Execution check: 300 Cr / 30 Cr capacity = 10 years to deliver ❌
Revenue recognition will be painfully slow
Risk of penalty clauses and cancellation is high

Verdict: TRAP ⚠️
The numbers look great on paper.
But if the company cannot execute at pace,
the order book is worthless paper.
Always cross-check order book vs physical capacity.
```

### Test Case 6 — Large Company Segment Trick (Reliance Rule)
```
Company   : Reliance Industries
Revenue   : ₹9,00,000 Cr (total)
Market Cap: ₹17,00,000 Cr
Order     : ₹5,000 Cr (green energy segment)

WRONG WAY (whole company):
Coverage Ratio : 5000 / 900000 = 0.006x → NOISE ❌
Market Cap %   : 5000 / 1700000 = 0.3%  → NOISE ❌

RIGHT WAY (segment only):
Green Energy Revenue : ₹5,000 Cr (new segment)
Coverage Ratio       : 5000 / 5000 = 1x  → MEANINGFUL ✅
Segment is new → weight it 2x            → SIGNIFICANT ✅

Verdict: IGNORE for Reliance itself
         But BUY for its SUPPLIERS 🟢
         Who supplies pipes, solar panels,
         cables, components to this project?
         Those smaller companies will re-rate.

Rule: For large conglomerates always
      test order vs SEGMENT revenue not total.
      Then find the supplier ecosystem.
```

## The 5 Pure Financial Points (Research Backed)

---

### Point 1 — Coverage Ratio is the Foundation
The order book to revenue ratio (also called book-to-bill ratio) is the single most used metric by analysts for project-driven companies. Sectors where this matters most: infrastructure, defence, capital goods, EPC, shipbuilding, manufacturing.

Real Indian benchmarks from research:
```
RBM Infracon     : 32.6x → exceptional (small company, massive pipeline)
Genus Power      : 15.9x → strong smart meter demand
Gensol Engg      : 8.1x  → solar EPC surge
Garden Reach     : 6.6x  → naval defence boom
BEL              : 3.6x  → defence electronics steady
HG Infra         : 2.8x  → road construction stable
```

---

### Point 2 — Order Book vs Market Cap is the Value Signal
When a company's order book exceeds its market cap, it is a classic deep value signal — the market has not priced in the committed revenue pipeline. This is common in infrastructure, defence, and heavy engineering where project cycles are long.

Real examples:
```
J. Kumar Infra   : Order book ₹21,011 Cr vs Market Cap ₹6,780 Cr = 310% ✅
Capacit'e Infra  : Order book ₹9,000 Cr vs Market Cap ₹2,548 Cr  = 353% ✅
```

---

### Point 3 — Order Book Quality > Order Book Size
A ₹12,000 Cr high-quality order book with fast execution beats a ₹20,000 Cr order book with poor payment discipline or low margins. Quality factors:

```
Client type     → Govt/Defence > PSU > Large Private > SME
Payment terms   → Fast milestone payment > slow payment cycles
Margin profile  → High margin orders > commodity/bid orders
Execution pace  → Fast-moving orders > slow-moving/legacy orders
```

L&T explicitly tracks "slow-moving orders" as a risk metric. As of June 2023, slow-moving orders were less than 1% of their book — a positive signal.

---

### Point 4 — Order Inflow Rate Matters as Much as Order Book Size
A static order book that isn't being refilled will shrink. The rate of new order inflow shows whether the pipeline is growing or depleting.

```
Order Inflow Rate = New Orders Won in Period / Revenue in Same Period

> 1.2x → Pipeline growing faster than execution → BULLISH ✅
= 1.0x → Pipeline stable → NEUTRAL ⚠️
< 0.8x → Orders depleting faster than refill → WARNING ❌
```

BEL example: Guided ₹27,000 Cr order inflow for FY26. Achieved 67% by 9 months → on track ✅

---

### Point 5 — Cash Flow is the Real Proof of Order Book Value
The most critical research finding: a large order book does not automatically mean shareholder value. The true test is whether orders convert to cash.

```
Key metrics to combine with order book:

1. Operating Cash Flow vs PAT
   → Are profits real or just accounting?

2. Cash Conversion Ratio = OCF / EBITDA
   → > 0.8 is healthy, < 0.5 is a red flag

3. Net Debt to EBITDA
   → Rising debt during order book growth = warning

4. Working Capital Days
   → Increasing receivables = client not paying

5. Interest Coverage Ratio
   → Must stay above 3x to be safe
```

As research shows: "A company growing revenue but consuming cash is increasing financial risk." Order book growth + cash flow deterioration = TRAP.

## The Decision Tree (Full Logic)

```
New order announced
        ↓
Step 1: Coverage Ratio = Order / Revenue
        < 1x → skip
        > 2x → proceed to Step 2
        ↓
Step 2: Market Cap % = Order / Market Cap × 100
        < 10% → NOISE, skip
        > 30% → significant, proceed to Step 3
        ↓
Step 3: Can the company execute?
        Order / Annual Capacity > 7 years → TRAP
        Order / Annual Capacity < 4 years → proceed to Step 4
        ↓
Step 4: Who is the client?
        Govt/Defence → HIGH quality, proceed
        Unknown Private → investigate payment terms
        ↓
Step 5: Sector tailwind check
        Google Trends spiking for this sector? → Multiplier ✅
        Sector in decline? → Discount the order
        ↓
Step 6: Cash flow check
        OCF / EBITDA > 0.8? → Real value ✅
        OCF / EBITDA < 0.5? → Accounting profit only ❌
        ↓
FINAL SIGNAL: BUY / HOLD / IGNORE
```
